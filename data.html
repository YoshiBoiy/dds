Instrument design

Methodology

We conducted moderated remote usability tests of the live Georgia DDS website using seven scenario tasks aligned to the official usability task list below (road test scheduling, Real ID preparation, lost-license replacement, locating a service center, disambiguating appointment versus online options, finding forms and manuals, and getting help). Tasks were counterbalanced where feasible to mitigate learning effects; each session included a think-aloud protocol with minimal facilitator prompting unless the participant explicitly abandoned a task for more than three minutes.

We recruited five participants (target band for rapid formative testing). Demographic capture was limited to what our IRB or course protocol permitted; per assignment guidance, this report omits demographic charts and focuses strictly on product performance. Sessions were recorded (screen and audio) under a signed consent process. Pre-session questions captured expectations, prior familiarity with DDS digital services, and comfort with similar government sites. Post-session questions captured perceived task difficulty, self-reported confidence on a 1-to-5 Likert scale, and brief system-usability-style items adapted for session length. Full wording lives in the linked instruments below.

Usability task list

Each scenario specified a realistic citizen goal and the primary site areas we expected participants to touch; facilitators scored strict success against these scripts.

  1. Road test scheduling — Learn how to schedule a Georgia road test and what to bring. Screens: homepage, appointments, road test requirements.
  2. Real ID requirements — Confirm which documents to bring so one trip suffices. Screens: Real ID information, document checklist.
  3. Replace a lost license — Steps and fees to replace a license, without completing payment. Screens: online services, license replacement, fees.
  4. Find a service center — Nearest customer service center, hours, and offered services. Screens: location finder, individual center page.
  5. Appointment vs. online — Determine whether replacement requires an in-person appointment or can be done online. Screens: online services, appointments, help/FAQ.
  6. Forms & manuals — Locate forms or manuals to review or bring for license-related services. Screens: forms, manuals, navigation menus.
  7. Getting help — Quickly find assistance for navigating the site and available services. Screens: help/contact, chatbot (if available).

Quantitative telemetry included binary task success (strict completion criteria per task script), time-on-task from prompt to correct destination or abandonment, first-click section accuracy (whether the initial navigation choice matched the optimal IA path we defined in a facilitator rubric), and assisted recovery count (number of times a facilitator hint was required). Qualitative data comprised utterance coding for confusion themes, annotated timelines tied to video timestamps, and post-task debrief prompts.

Tools included browser screen sharing, spreadsheet logging, and a lightweight rubric for “information discovered late” when participants opened the correct policy text only after extended scrolling or after visiting a parallel section (for example, bouncing between Appointments and Driver Services). Figures in the Findings section visualize performance aggregates; the quote table preserves participant language tied to observed behaviors.

Observed performance

Findings

Aggregate success rates indicate that participants could eventually reach correct destinations on most tasks, but several tasks exhibited low strict success within the allotted window, most notably the lost-license replacement path, where multi-page traversal and ambiguous entry points produced repeated loops. The first bar chart summarizes per-task success for the seven scenarios.

Time-on-task told a complementary story: even successful completions often arrived after extended scanning. The line chart highlights tasks where median duration exceeded our facilitator target of ninety seconds for lookup tasks and one hundred twenty seconds for multi-step initiation tasks. Long durations correlated with late discovery of checklists and PDFs buried beneath general explanatory copy.

First-click accuracy exposed a structural issue: participants frequently entered a plausible but incorrect top-level section because labels were perceived as overlapping. The third visualization encodes the proportion of sessions where the first navigation choice was suboptimal for the task’s ideal path. High values on appointments-versus-services confusion support redesign of label semantics and cross-links.

Post-test confidence remained mid-range despite eventual success on several tasks, suggesting that efficiency and certainty, not mere reachability, drive perceived quality. The fourth chart plots mean confidence by task family (scheduling, compliance research, replacement, location discovery).

Finally, we tracked late document discovery events per session (instances where required documents were encountered only after the participant had already invested substantial time in the wrong content band). The horizontal bar chart shows the count of such events across tasks; Real ID and replacement tasks dominate. The table beneath collects verbatim frustration signals aligned to those behavioral codes.

Synthesis

Analysis

Taken together, the success and latency charts argue against a simple missing-content diagnosis. Participants frequently reached policy-adjacent pages yet still violated strict success rules because success required timely identification of a checklist or PDF anchor. As seen in the horizontal bar chart for late discovery, Real ID and replacement tasks concentrate coded events, consistent with screen evidence where users scrolled past headings before returning after facilitator probes.

The first-click inaccuracy visualization supports a targeted IA intervention: when users begin in the wrong top-level silo, they pay a nonlinear tax in backtracking. Our prototype therefore differentiates intent-led entry (“I need to…”) from departmental taxonomy, while preserving deep links for power users. This is not cosmetic; it directly addresses the measured 80% inaccuracy cluster for scheduling versus services ambiguity.

The confidence chart explains why mid-range satisfaction is compatible with eventual completion. Users who succeed late still encode the experience as uncertain, which is risky for a domain where errors produce real-world costs (return trips, denied transactions). Designs that surface what-to-bring modules adjacent to initiation buttons address both the latency curve (Fig. 1.2) and the psychological outcome (Fig. 1.4).

Qualitative quotes operationalize the quantitative codes. Participant 4’s statement about parallel doors mirrors the first-click metric; Participant 2’s comment about buried lists motivates checklist modules adjacent to task entry, not buried PDF anchors alone. These statements are not anecdotal outliers; they triangulate repeated behavioral markers across at least three sessions for navigation ambiguity.

Finally, the lost-license task’s combination of low strict success (Fig. 1.1) and high median time (Fig. 1.2) justifies a guided, step-visible flow rather than incremental copy edits. Engineering should treat the wizard pattern as a structured state machine with resumable checkpoints, not as a marketing carousel; the analysis implies measurable gains if each step declares prerequisites before routing users to scheduling.
