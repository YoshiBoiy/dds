Georgia DDS Digital Services

Methodology

We ran moderated remote usability tests of the live Georgia DDS website with 6 participants who matched our target audience—Georgia residents, mostly students and early-career workers. Each session was about 15 to 20 minutes and followed a think-aloud format, where participants talked through what they were doing and looking for as they worked through each task. We only stepped in with a prompt if a participant gave up on a task or got completely stuck for more than a few minutes.

We gave each participant 7 realistic tasks based on common things people actually do on the DDS website: scheduling a road test, finding Real ID requirements, replacing a lost license, locating a service center, figuring out whether a task needs an in-person appointment or can be done online, finding forms and manuals, and getting help. None of the tasks asked for sensitive information or required logging in. Before each session, a pre-test survey captured expectations and prior familiarity with DDS or similar government sites. After each session, a post-test survey asked about perceived difficulty and a 1-to-5 confidence rating per task. Most survey items received 6 responses; the mobile-responsiveness item received 5 responses.

Usability task list

Each scenario specified a realistic citizen goal and the primary site areas we expected participants to touch; facilitators scored strict success against these scripts.

  1. Road test scheduling — Learn how to schedule a Georgia road test and what to bring. Screens: homepage, appointments, road test requirements.
  2. Real ID requirements — Confirm which documents to bring so one trip suffices. Screens: Real ID information, document checklist.
  3. Replace a lost license — Steps and fees to replace a license, without completing payment. Screens: online services, license replacement, fees.
  4. Find a service center — Nearest customer service center, hours, and offered services. Screens: location finder, individual center page.
  5. Appointment vs. online — Determine whether replacement requires an in-person appointment or can be done online. Screens: online services, appointments, help/FAQ.
  6. Forms & manuals — Locate forms or manuals to review or bring for license-related services. Screens: forms, manuals, navigation menus.
  7. Getting help — Quickly find assistance for navigating the site and available services. Screens: help/contact, chatbot (if available).

For each task we tracked: task success (did they complete it correctly within the time limit), time-on-task (how long they spent), first-click accuracy (was their first navigation choice the right section), and how many times they needed a hint from the facilitator. We also coded the recordings for confusion moments—times when a participant opened the wrong page, scrolled past the right answer, or explicitly said they were lost. We pulled quotes from the recordings to tie specific frustrations to specific behaviors.

Tools were pretty simple: browser screen sharing with audio recording, a shared spreadsheet for logging, and a lightweight rubric for marking “late document discovery” when a participant found a required checklist or PDF only after wasting time elsewhere (for example, bouncing between Appointments and Driver Services). The charts in the Findings section summarize how the group performed across tasks, and the quote table below captures how participants described what went wrong in their own words.

Findings

The post-test data shows that participants could generally complete DDS tasks, but the experience still required effort and clearer navigation. In the overall ease rating, 66.7% of respondents called the tasks easy and 33.3% were neutral; no one rated the tasks difficult. That positive result was tempered by information-architecture issues: 100% of respondents said they looked first to the main navigation menu or tabs, while no respondents reported starting with the search bar. On the homepage, only 50% felt the amount of information was about right, while 33.3% said there was too much information and 16.7% said there was too little. The services people expected to use most were scheduling/canceling appointments (83.3%) and license renewal or replacement (66.7%), which supports making those actions more prominent. Confidence was mixed: responses split across ratings 2, 3, 4, and 5, with no one choosing 1. Error messages and prompts also landed mostly in the fair-to-good range, while the mobile-responsiveness item skewed positive with 60% rating it 4 out of 5.

Analysis

Users were generally able to complete tasks, but the experience still required effort. In the task ease chart, 66.7% of participants rated tasks as easy and 33.3% as neutral, with no one reporting difficulty. While this suggests the website is functional, the spread in confidence ratings tells a different story. Users often finished tasks without feeling fully certain they did them correctly, pointing to unclear navigation and limited guidance.

Navigation issues appeared most consistently across the data. All participants started with the main navigation menu, while none used the search bar, showing a strong reliance on menu labels. However, those labels were often confusing. Sections like “Appointments,” “Driver Services,” and “License/ID” felt too similar, leading users to click the wrong option first and backtrack. This pattern explains the low first-click accuracy and directly motivated the redesign to use clearer, more distinct categories.

The homepage also made it harder for users to quickly find what they needed. Only half of participants felt the amount of information was balanced, while others found it either overwhelming or lacking. The issue was less about quantity and more about structure. Key actions were not clearly prioritized, forcing users to click through multiple sections. To address this, common tasks like scheduling appointments and renewing licenses were moved to more visible positions on the homepage.

Usage data highlights what users actually care about. Most participants expected to use appointment scheduling (83.3%) and license renewal or replacement (66.7%), while none selected forms and publications. This gap shows that the original design emphasized less relevant content. The redesign shifts focus toward these high-demand tasks by making them easy to access directly, instead of hiding them behind navigation layers.

Another key issue was finding required documents. Many participants struggled to locate what they needed to bring and often discovered this information too late. This caused delays and frustration during tasks. The problem was not missing content, but poor placement. By adding document lists, fees, and deadlines directly onto service pages, the redesign helps users gather everything they need in one place.

Smaller usability gaps also appeared in help and feedback. Error messages were rated mostly fair to good, suggesting they could be clearer. Some participants also had trouble finding help when needed. Mobile responsiveness performed reasonably well, though not perfectly. Together, these findings point to a need for simpler layouts, clearer messaging, and more visible support options. Overall, the main issues came down to navigation clarity, organization, and task visibility, all of which are directly addressed in the redesign.
