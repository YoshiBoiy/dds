Home Data Recommendations

Recommendations

Our recommendations turn the data on the previous page into specific changes to the site’s navigation, layout, and interactions. Each one targets a measurable problem we saw in testing and connects to a specific usability attribute.

Before and after comparisons

1. Replace hero image with Quick and Popular Services

The original homepage had a large image at the top that took up most of the screen but did not help users complete tasks. Important actions like renewing a license or scheduling an appointment were pushed down the page, so users had to scroll or search to find them. Our redesign removes the large image and adds Quick Services and Popular Services at the top of the page. These sections clearly show common actions and make it easier to scan and choose a task. This makes the site more efficient and easier to use because users can start tasks right away.

2. Improve Locations page with search and direct actions

The original Locations page used long lists and links that required users to click through multiple pages to find information. Users could not directly schedule appointments and there was no easy way to search for a location. Our redesign adds a search bar at the top, structured location cards, and a Schedule button for each location. An interactive map is also included. This makes the page more efficient because users can find and act on locations in one place.

3. Replace bullet lists with structured sections and key info

The original License/ID page relied on long bullet lists and links, making it hard to understand what steps to take. Important details like required documents, pricing, and deadlines were not clearly visible. Our redesign replaces this with structured sections and boxes for each license type. It also adds required documents, pricing, and the REAL ID deadline directly on the page. This makes the page clearer and helps users avoid missing important information.

4. Reorganize Testing into Appointments and add Vehicle Services page

The original Testing and Training page grouped many different links together, making it hard to find specific actions like scheduling a road test. Vehicle-related services were also not clearly grouped. Our redesign moves testing actions into a clear Appointments section and creates a dedicated Vehicle Services page. This makes the site more organized and helps users quickly find the correct section based on their goal.

5. Replace Forms page with Online Services

The original Forms and Manuals page focused on lists of documents, which did not clearly guide users toward completing tasks. Many users still had to visit the DMV even when tasks could be done online. Our redesign replaces this with an Online Services page that shows actions users can complete from home, with clear buttons and pricing. This makes the site more convenient and reduces unnecessary in-person visits.

Closing argument

Conclusion

We realized that for a site like DDS, the biggest usability issues aren’t about missing features or visual polish—they’re about how information is organized and how users find their way through it. Participants were generally patient with a plain-looking interface, but they weren’t patient with ambiguity about which section to try first or where a required document was hiding. Our process combined quick quantitative measurements (task success, time, first-click accuracy) with qualitative notes and quotes, which let us point to specific parts of the UI with real evidence instead of opinions.

Next steps for a real production rollout would include A/B testing the new navigation labels on a subset of users, adding analytics to track first-click paths after the redesign, and running an accessibility audit on the stepper and locator components. We’d also want to set up a process so that the checklist modules stay in sync with policy updates as DDS rules change.

Engineering Hand-off: our prototype is meant as a high-fidelity mock-up of how the DDS website should look after addressing current UX painpoints rather than a production-ready website. A few changes would help turn this into a clean implementation. For instance, treating the new top-level navigation (home, driver’s license, vehicle services, online services, locations, and appointments) as the single source of truth is key to to make navigating the DDS website simple. Setting up redirects from old URLs to these new top-level URLs would be important during the migration, so that existing bookmarks and search results would still work for users. In addition, building the document checklists, fees, and Real ID deadlines as structured content rather than hard-coded text is important such that they can be updated in on place as DDS policy changes over time. Adding basic clickthrough analytics would be helpful to measure whether the redesign actually aligns with the way in which users use the DDS website. Finally, running the new pages through an accessibility check would ensure that the website remains widely accessible to the general population, as intended. We are happy to walk through the Figma files with the engineering team and answer any questions during implementation.
