Home Data Recommendations

Recommendations

Our recommendations turn the data on the previous page into specific changes to the site’s navigation, layout, and interactions. Each one targets a measurable problem we saw in testing and connects to a specific usability attribute.

1. Replace the hero image with Quick and Popular Services. The original homepage used a large image at the top that took up most of the screen but did not help users complete tasks. Important actions like renewing a license or scheduling an appointment were pushed down the page, so users had to scroll or search to find them. This made the site slower and more frustrating to use. Our redesign removes the large image and adds Quick Services and Popular Services at the top of the page. These sections clearly show common actions like license renewal, vehicle registration, and scheduling appointments, making it easy to scan and choose a task. This makes the site more efficient and easier to use— users can find and start tasks right away. The trade-off is less visual emphasis at the top of the page, but this is acceptable because it improves usability and helps users complete their goals faster.

2. Put document checklists next to the action they go with. Real ID and lost-license tasks had the most late-discovery events, because the list of required documents is usually inside long paragraphs or hidden in a linked PDF. Our prototype puts a clearly boxed checklist right next to buttons like “Start replacement” or “Prepare for your visit.” This cuts down on time-on-task and improves error tolerance—users are much less likely to show up missing a document. The maintenance trade-off is that the same information appears in more places, which we’d solve by pulling from a shared data source so a single update changes every spot.

Closing argument

Conclusion

We realized that for a site like DDS, the biggest usability issues aren’t about missing features or visual polish—they’re about how information is organized and how users find their way through it. Participants were generally patient with a plain-looking interface, but they weren’t patient with ambiguity about which section to try first or where a required document was hiding. Our process combined quick quantitative measurements (task success, time, first-click accuracy) with qualitative notes and quotes, which let us point to specific parts of the UI with real evidence instead of opinions.

Next steps for a real production rollout would include A/B testing the new navigation labels on a subset of users, adding analytics to track first-click paths after the redesign, and running an accessibility audit on the stepper and locator components. We’d also want to set up a process so that the checklist modules stay in sync with policy updates as DDS rules change.
