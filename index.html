Improving discoverability on Georgia’s DDS website

Group 5: Joel David Sari, Tanya Khurana, Tejas Khare, Vinay Gupta, Yash Mahendra Shelar

Executive digest

Summary

The Georgia Department of Driver Services (DDS) website supports high-stakes tasks—appointments, renewals, Real ID preparation, and replacements—yet our moderated usability sessions showed that correct information was frequently present but poorly surfaced. Participants often eventually succeeded, but only after redundant scanning, backtracking between similarly labeled areas (Appointments, Driver Services, and License/ID), and slow recovery when document requirements appeared deep in long-form pages.

We executed seven task-based scenarios spanning scheduling, compliance research, loss replacement, and center location with five participants. Behavioral metrics—completion, time-on-task, error counts, and self-reported confidence—clustered in a mid-range band, signaling a service that is usable in principle but not efficient in practice. Observations consistently pointed to information architecture and progressive disclosure problems rather than missing content.

Our high-fidelity prototype preserves comprehensive policy detail while reorganizing the interface around primary citizen intents, clearer section semantics, and guided flows for multi-step processes (notably lost-license replacement). The design intent is to reduce wrong-section entries, shorten document discovery time, and increase first-attempt confidence.

Key takeaway: stakeholders should treat navigation labeling, task-level checklists, and guided process templates as first-class product requirements for citizen-facing government services. The Data page quantifies where friction concentrates; the Recommendations page maps each issue to a concrete UI response and an interactive Figma prototype suitable for engineering review.

Orientation

Introduction

Digital channels for motor-safety administration carry an unusual burden: they must remain legally precise while remaining legible to distracted users under time pressure. Georgia’s DDS web presence is a critical state service for millions of licensed drivers; small navigation failures compound into missed appointments, unprepared visits, and support load. Our team selected this product because its scale, regulatory complexity, and heterogeneous users create a representative case for enterprise information architecture, where completeness and clarity are often assumed to trade against one another.

The target user demographic is Georgia residents who need a license or ID service under time pressure—students, early-career and working adults, and new drivers—rather than DMV specialists. Our working persona, “Jordan,” is a moderately tech-comfortable college student or early-career worker who typically arrives on the site from a phone, late at night or between classes and shifts, looking for a direct answer. Users in this band are not fluent in departmental terminology; they carry task identities (“renew my license,” “get Real ID,” “replace a lost card”) and expect the interface to translate those intents into next steps. This framing informed our task selection and severity judgments throughout the study.

The current site exposes a large taxonomy of services. In practice, citizens arrive with task identities rather than department-internal categories, and when top-level labels overlap in meaning, users must guess which silo contains their answer. We hypothesized that this structural ambiguity would inflate exploratory clicking, increase time-on-task variance, and depress confidence even when answers exist on-page. Early heuristic review reinforced the concern: a crowded homepage of announcements, parallel navigation categories, and multiple redundant paths to the same action (Online Services, forms/manuals, chatbot) suggested an organizational rather than content problem.

We further hypothesized that document requirements, which carry high consequence if misunderstood, were vulnerable to late discovery because they frequently appeared inside dense paragraphs rather than adjacent to the action they gate. Finally, we expected multi-page processes (for example, replacement after loss) to show higher abandonment or repeated navigation loops, because users lack a visible progress model tying steps together.

This report documents how those hypotheses were operationalized into tasks and metrics, what the mixed-methods evidence showed, and how the prototype responds without sacrificing the comprehensiveness stakeholders require. The tone is deliberately analytical: our goal is not stylistic preference but measurable task improvement justified by observed behavior and participant statements.
