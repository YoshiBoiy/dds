Technical report

Improving discoverability on Georgia’s DDS website

Group 5: Joel David Sari, Tanya Khurana, Tejas Khare, Vinay Gupta, Yash Mahendra Shelar

Summary

The Georgia DDS website is where people go to handle high-stakes tasks like scheduling a road test, renewing or replacing a license or ID, finding the documents needed for Real ID, paying fees, and locating a service center. From our usability study, we found that most of the information users need is already there, but it’s often hard to find when you’re in a hurry. Most participants finished their tasks eventually, but only after bouncing between overlapping sections like Appointments, Driver Services, and License/ID, or scanning through long blocks of text to find required documents.

We tested the site with 6 participants using 7 realistic tasks, covering scheduling, Real ID research, replacing a lost license, and locating a service center. We tracked task success, time-on-task, misclicks, and confidence ratings. Across the board, scores landed in the mid-range—the site works, but it’s not efficient. Almost all of the confusion we saw came from how the information is organized, not from missing content.

Our high-fidelity prototype keeps the site’s full detail but reorganizes it around what users are actually trying to do. We simplified the top-level navigation, moved document checklists next to the action they go with, and built a step-by-step flow for the lost-license replacement process. The goal is to reduce wrong-section clicks, help people find documents faster, and make users more confident on their first try.

Key takeaway: for government sites like DDS, clearer labels, task-level checklists, and guided flows should be treated as core requirements, not extras. The Data page shows where users got stuck and why; the Recommendations page walks through each change we made and links to our interactive Figma prototype.

Orientation

Introduction

The Georgia DDS website is a critical state service. Over 7 million people in Georgia have a driver’s license, and most of them eventually need to use this site for something—renewing a license, scheduling a road test, finding out what documents they need, or locating a service center. When the site is hard to navigate, the cost isn’t just frustration. It means missed appointments, unprepared visits, and extra calls to customer service. We chose DDS because it’s a real, high-traffic service that people depend on, and small navigation problems add up across millions of users.

Our target users are Georgia residents who need a license or ID service, especially people who are under time pressure—students, working adults, and new drivers. Most of them aren’t DMV experts. They’re trying to finish something quickly on their phone or laptop, often late at night or between classes and work. Our working persona, “Jordan,” is a college student or early-career worker who’s moderately comfortable with technology but not familiar with DMV terminology. Jordan wants a clear answer fast, without having to read through pages of policy text.

Going in, we had a few hypotheses about what might go wrong. The homepage has a lot of announcements, news, and overlapping navigation categories (Appointments, Driver Services, License/ID), which we thought would make users guess the wrong section on their first click. We also thought that required documents for Real ID and license replacement would be hard to find because they’re usually buried inside long paragraphs instead of shown next to the action they go with. And for multi-step tasks like replacing a lost license, we expected users to lose track of where they were because there’s no clear progress indicator.

This report walks through how we tested those hypotheses, what we found, and how our prototype addresses the issues. We focused on measurable problems—task success rates, time-on-task, first-click accuracy—so that the design decisions aren’t just opinions but are based on what we actually saw users do and say.
