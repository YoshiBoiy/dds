Technical report · usability engineering

Improving discoverability on Georgia’s DDS website

Authors (Group 5): [Yash Shelar], [Vinay Gupta], [Joel Sari], [Tejas Khare], [Tanya Khurana]

Executive digest

Summary

The Georgia Department of Driver Services (DDS) website supports high-stakes tasks—appointments, renewals, Real ID preparation, and replacements—yet our moderated usability sessions showed that correct information was frequently present but poorly surfaced. Participants could often eventually succeed, but the path required redundant scanning, backtracking between similarly labeled areas (for example, Appointments, Driver Services, and License/ID), and slow recovery when document requirements appeared deep in long-form pages.

We executed seven task-based scenarios spanning scheduling, compliance research, loss replacement, and center location. Behavioral metrics (completion, time-on-task, error counts, and self-reported confidence) clustered in a mid-range band, signaling a service that is usable in principle but not efficient in practice. Qualitative notes and screen observations consistently pointed to information architecture and progressive disclosure problems rather than missing content.

Our high-fidelity prototype preserves comprehensive policy detail while reorganizing the interface around primary citizen intents, clearer section semantics, and guided flows for multi-step processes (notably lost-license replacement). The design intent is to reduce wrong-section entries, shorten document discovery time, and increase confidence for first-time completion.

The study’s implications extend beyond visual polish: DDS serves millions of drivers in Georgia, so incremental friction aggregates into measurable social cost (unprepared visits, delayed renewals, and support demand). By pairing strict behavioral criteria with participant language, we aimed to produce evidence that product and engineering leaders can trace from chart to interface control.

Key takeaway: Stakeholders should treat navigation labeling, task-level checklists, and guided process templates as first-class product requirements for citizen-facing government services. The evidence package on the Data page quantifies where friction concentrates; the Recommendations page maps each issue to a concrete UI response and an interactive Figma prototype suitable for engineering review.

Orientation

Introduction

Digital channels for motor-safety administration carry an unusual burden: they must remain legally precise while remaining legible to distracted users under time pressure. Georgia’s DDS web presence is a critical state service for millions of licensed drivers; small navigation failures compound into missed appointments, unprepared visits, and support load. Our team selected this product because its scale, regulatory complexity, and heterogeneous users create a representative case for enterprise information architecture, where completeness and clarity are often assumed to trade against one another.

The current site exposes a large taxonomy of services. In practice, citizens arrive with task identities (“I lost my license,” “I need Real ID proof,” “I need a road test”) rather than department-internal categories. When top-level labels overlap in meaning, users must guess which silo contains their answer. We hypothesized that this structural ambiguity would inflate exploratory clicking, increase time-on-task variance, and depress confidence even when answers exist on-page.

We further hypothesized that document requirements, which carry high consequence if misunderstood, were vulnerable to late discovery because they frequently appeared inside dense paragraphs rather than adjacent to the action they gate. Finally, we expected multi-page processes (for example, replacement after loss) to show higher abandonment or repeated navigation loops, because users lack a visible progress model tying steps together.

This report documents how those hypotheses were operationalized into tasks and metrics, what the mixed-methods evidence showed, and how the prototype responds without sacrificing the comprehensiveness stakeholders require. The tone is deliberately analytical: our goal is not stylistic preference but measurable task improvement justified by observed behavior and participant statements.
